The State of Our Oceans

Our oceans cover 71% of the Earth's surface and support all life on our planet. Yet today they face threats more serious than ever before.

Why Oceans Matter

The ocean is often called the "lungs of the Earth." It produces over 50% of the world's oxygen and absorbs roughly a third of the carbon dioxide humans produce. Without healthy oceans, life on land would not survive.

Oceans also regulate our climate, supply food to billions of people, and support a vast web of species — from microscopic plankton to blue whales. SDG 14 (Life Below Water) exists because keeping oceans healthy is not optional — it is essential.

"The ocean is the cornerstone of Earth's life support system. It shapes climate, weather, and the chemical composition of the atmosphere."

Plastic Pollution

Every minute, the equivalent of a full rubbish truck of plastic enters our oceans. Most plastic does not biodegrade — it breaks into microplastics, tiny particles that enter the food chain and have been found in fish, seabirds, and even human blood.

Key facts:

  • Over 1 million seabirds and 100,000 marine mammals die from plastic pollution each year.
  • There are five large "garbage patches" in the world's oceans — the largest is twice the size of Texas.
  • Single-use plastics — straws, bags, bottles — make up the majority of ocean plastic.

Ocean Warming and Acidification

Oceans absorb more than 90% of excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases. This causes sea temperatures to rise, disrupting ecosystems that took millions of years to evolve.

When oceans absorb CO2, they become more acidic — a process called ocean acidification. This dissolves the shells of crabs, oysters, and corals, collapsing the food web from the bottom up.

  • 50% of coral reefs have been lost in the last 30 years due to bleaching.
  • Sea levels are rising at over 3mm per year, threatening coastal communities.
  • Warmer oceans produce stronger, more destructive storms and hurricanes.
Coral reefs support 25% of all marine life despite covering less than 1% of the ocean floor. Their loss would be catastrophic.

Overfishing

Humans remove fish from the sea faster than populations can reproduce. Today, more than 1 in 3 fish stocks are harvested at biologically unsustainable levels, according to the UN Food and Agriculture Organisation.

Industrial fishing fleets use methods like bottom trawling — dragging enormous nets across the seabed — which destroys habitats and catches thousands of non-target species (called "bycatch"), including dolphins, turtles, and sharks.

  • Around 3 billion people depend on seafood as their primary protein source.
  • If current trends continue, some species could collapse entirely within our lifetime.
  • Sustainable fishing practices can allow fish stocks to recover within years.
