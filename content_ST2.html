Ocean Biodiversity

The ocean is home to an estimated 700,000 to 1 million species, though scientists believe up to two-thirds of marine life has yet to be discovered. From microscopic plankton to 30-metre blue whales, this biodiversity keeps our entire planet functioning.

Marine ecosystems — coral reefs, mangroves, seagrass beds, and the deep sea — are deeply interconnected. When one species disappears, it creates a ripple effect through the entire food web, often in ways we cannot predict.

The ocean provides ecosystem services worth an estimated $2.5 trillion every year — food, oxygen, climate regulation, and cultural value for billions of people.

Key Threats to Marine Life

Marine animals face a combination of pressures, many of which are caused directly by human activity:

  • Pollution — Plastic, chemicals, agricultural runoff, and noise pollution disrupt feeding, reproduction, and navigation for countless species.
  • Climate change — Rising temperatures and acidification destroy habitats and shift the distribution of prey, forcing animals to migrate or starve.
  • Overfishing — Unsustainable fishing removes fish faster than they can breed, collapsing food webs that took thousands of years to develop.
  • Habitat destruction — Coastal development, dredging, and bottom trawling physically destroy the seabed habitats animals depend on.
  • Illegal wildlife trade — Species like sea turtles, seahorses, and sharks are hunted illegally for food, medicine, and the exotic pet trade.

Endangered Species to Know

These are some of the most well-known marine species currently facing extinction due to human pressure. Each plays a vital role in its ecosystem.

Endangered

Sea Turtle

Threatened by plastic ingestion, fishing nets, and beach development that destroys nesting sites.

Vulnerable

Blue Whale

The largest animal on Earth, still recovering from commercial whaling. Now threatened by ship strikes and noise pollution.

Endangered

Great White Shark

Killed for their fins and caught as bycatch. As apex predators, their loss destabilises entire ocean ecosystems.

Vulnerable

Seahorse

Over 37 million seahorses are traded each year for traditional medicine and the aquarium trade.

The Crisis Facing Coral Reefs

Coral reefs are sometimes called the "rainforests of the sea." They cover less than 1% of the ocean floor yet support around 25% of all marine species. Without them, the ocean food web collapses.

When oceans warm, corals expel the algae living in their tissues and turn completely white — a process called coral bleaching. Bleached coral is not dead yet, but if temperatures stay high it will die. Mass bleaching events now happen five times more often than in the 1980s.

  • The Great Barrier Reef has experienced six mass bleaching events since 1998.
  • Scientists warn that at 2°C of warming, 99% of coral reefs could be lost.
  • Around 500 million people depend on reefs for food, income, and coastal protection.
Protecting coral reefs is not just about saving beautiful wildlife — it is about protecting the livelihoods and food security of half a billion people worldwide.

How We Can Help

Protecting marine life does not require being a marine biologist. As students and everyday citizens, these three actions make a real difference:

  • Say no to single-use plastic. Straws, bags, and bottles are the leading items found in turtle stomachs. Switching to reusables directly saves lives.
  • Support sustainable seafood. Choose fish caught or farmed responsibly. The Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) label is a simple guide to look for at supermarkets and restaurants.
  • Spread awareness. Share what you learn with family and friends. Consumer pressure and public awareness have driven real policy changes — from plastic bag bans to shark fin trade restrictions.
Every choice we make as consumers — what we eat, what we buy, what we throw away — has a direct impact on life below water.
